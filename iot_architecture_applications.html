Architecture of IoT


  1. Sensors/Actuators - are the actual objects that interact with the environment to gather data.

  2. Device/Gateway Layer - This layer is in charge of providing data storage, processing, and communication capabilities as well as linking the sensors and actuators to the internet.

  3. Network Layer - This layer provides the communication infrastructure for the IoT system.

  4. Platform Layer - This layer provides the software and hardware infrastructure for the IoT system.

  5. Application Layer - This layer consists of the applications and services that make use of the data collected by the IoT system.

Application of IoT


    The versatile nature of IoT makes it an attractive option for so many businesses, organizations, and government branches, that it doesn’t make sense to ignore it. Here’s a sample of various industries, and how IoT can be best applied.

  1. Agriculture - For indoor planting, IoT makes monitoring and management of micro- climate conditions a reality, which in turn increases production. For outside planting, devices using IoT technology can sense soil moisture and nutrients, in conjunction with weather data, better control smart irrigation and fertilizer systems.

  2. Consumer Use - TFor private citizens, IoT devices in the form of wearables and smart homes make life easier. Wearables cover accessories such as Fitbit, smartphones, Apple watches, health monitors, to name a few. These devices improve entertainment, network connectivity, health, and fitness.

  3. Healthcare - First and foremost, wearable IoT devices let hospitals monitor their patients’ health at home, thereby reducing hospital stays while still providing up to the minute real-time information that could save lives. In hospitals, smart beds keep the staff informed as to the availability, thereby cutting wait time for free space.

  4. Insurance - Insurance companies can offer their policyholders discounts for IoT wearables such as Fitbit. By employing fitness tracking, the insurer can offer customized policies and encourage healthier habits, which in the long run benefits everyone, insurer, and customer alike.

  5. Manufacturing - The world of manufacturing and industrial automation is another big winner in the IoT sweepstakes. RFID and GPS technology can help a manufacturer track a product from its start on the factory floor to its placement in the destination store, the whole supply chain from start to finish.
  6. Retail - IoT sensors can help retailers control warehouse automation and robotics, analyze mall traffic, target customers based on past purchases, and craft personalized promotions for loyal customers.
  7. Transportation - GPS is being used to help transportation companies plot faster and more efficient routes, and city planners can use it to determine traffic patterns, parking space demand, and road construction and maintenance.
  8. Utilities - IoT sensors can be used to monitor environmental conditions such as humidity, temperature, and lighting, allowing algorithms to regulate energy usage and make adjustments. This allows businesses and private residences to experience significant energy savings, which benefits everyone, including the environment. Data gathered by IoT sensors can also be used to help run municipal power grids more efficiently and pinpoint outages faster, increasing the response time of repair crews and decreasing blackout times.
